# Survey 1 Knowledge Base — Brand Trust and Information Sources of the TA

## Purpose
This document consolidates Survey 1 into a reusable knowledge base for the project.  
It summarizes how the TA:
- finds and trusts information
- chooses brands across major life categories
- defines quality, service, durability, and premium
- rejects brands and loses trust
- evaluates whether a brand feels safe, mature, and worth buying

This KB is meant to support later work on:
- TA persona updates
- Discovery Ads semantic core updates
- landing page trust architecture
- creative direction
- messaging and premium-positioning decisions

---

## 1. Executive summary

### Core truth
The TA is **mainstream-trusting, credibility-sensitive, and familiarity-led**.

They are not anti-premium, but they do not respond best to loud, status-heavy, or experimental brands.  
Their strongest trust goes to brands that feel:
- proven
- stable
- widely recognized
- dependable in everyday life
- easy to understand
- low-friction to own

### Most important strategic conclusion
For this TA, the best version of “premium” is:

**well-made, trustworthy, quietly confident, and easy to live with**

Not:
- flashy luxury
- novelty tech
- edgy disruption
- “premium because it is expensive”

### Strongest universal trust signals
Across Survey 1, the TA repeatedly rewards brands that feel:
- mainstream but strong
- dependable over time
- supported by good customer service
- durable / built to last
- mature and understated
- practical rather than performative

### Strongest trust breakers
The biggest negative triggers are:
- poor quality / doesn’t last
- ethical or values conflict
- bad past experience
- distrust
- price not justified by value
- poor customer service

## 2. Information-source behavior

## News & politics
The TA’s news behavior is:
- mainstream-first
- aggregator-assisted
- credibility-sensitive

They most often use major TV/news brands and local news, while also using a meaningful layer of AP / Reuters / BBC / NPR-style sources and aggregators such as Google News and Apple News.

**Meaning for brand strategy:** broad discovery can happen in familiar environments, but trust is strengthened by recognizable and established names.

## Sports
Sports behavior is:
- more brand-concentrated
- more habit-driven
- more official-source friendly

The TA often starts with major sports media brands, then moves toward official league, team, or event sources when interest is higher.

**Meaning for brand strategy:** when involvement goes up, direct and category-authoritative proof becomes more important.

## Technology & gadgets
Tech behavior is:
- research-led
- utility-driven
- multi-source

The TA combines:
- review publishers
- YouTube
- search
- official websites
- some Reddit / social / AI tools

**Meaning for brand strategy:** tech-adjacent purchases need a layered trust stack, not just one source.

## Health & wellness
Health behavior is:
- credibility-seeking
- practical
- routine-oriented

The TA combines:
- medical institutions
- familiar reference brands like WebMD and Mayo Clinic
- daily-use fitness / tracking tools

**Meaning for brand strategy:** in personal or higher-stakes categories, reassurance must feel credible and practical.

## Food & cooking
Food behavior is:
- convenience-led
- visually inspired
- familiarity-driven

The TA uses mainstream recipe brands, visual discovery channels, and a smaller authority-cooking layer.

**Meaning for brand strategy:** in low-stakes lifestyle categories, inspiration and familiarity can carry more weight than institutional authority.

## Finance & investing
Finance behavior is:
- trust-heavy
- decision-support oriented
- institutionally anchored

The TA mixes:
- account-linked financial platforms
- major business media
- planning / guidance brands
- lightweight market-tracking tools

**Meaning for brand strategy:** expensive purchases need a trust stack that feels serious, validated, and consequence-aware.

## 3. Category purchase behavior

## Electronics / tech
The TA is highly concentrated around:
- Apple as the dominant brand
- Samsung as the main alternative
- a smaller familiar set for computing, audio, and home entertainment

**Signal:** they narrow choices to a small trusted shortlist and are comfortable with ecosystem-based decisions.

## Clothing / footwear
The TA is practical, comfort-aware, and anchored in:
- Nike
- Adidas
- New Balance
- Brooks
- Skechers
- Clarks

**Signal:** fit, comfort, reliability, and everyday usability matter more than trend chasing.

## Home / appliances
The TA is strongly concentrated around:
- LG
- GE
- Whirlpool
- Samsung
- KitchenAid
- Bosch

**Signal:** home purchases are pragmatic and trust-shortlist driven. Reliability and long-term household confidence matter more than novelty.

## Health / fitness
The category is split between:
- wearable / tracking ecosystems
- supplements / wellness maintenance

Key anchors include Fitbit, Apple, Garmin, GNC, Nature Made, and Peloton.

**Signal:** this TA sees health/fintess purchases as useful tools for better daily living, not mainly as identity or status signals.

## Travel
The TA relies most on:
- major airline brands
- major hotel brands
- mainstream booking platforms

American Airlines, Southwest, Delta, United, Hilton, Marriott, and Hyatt dominate.

**Signal:** in service-risk categories, the TA narrows into well-known and predictable brands.

## Automotive
Automotive behavior is:
- practical
- familiarity-led
- grounded in long-term ownership and upkeep

Toyota and Ford are major anchors, followed by Nissan, Subaru, Hyundai, Kia, and Honda.

**Signal:** this TA values dependable ownership, maintenance confidence, and serviceability.

## 6. Unified model of how the TA makes trust decisions

Across the whole survey, the TA appears to follow a consistent pattern:

### Step 1: Discovery can happen broadly
Discovery can come from:
- mainstream media
- search
- YouTube
- aggregators
- familiar digital environments
- social in some low-stakes or inspiration-led categories

### Step 2: Trust narrows fast
Once stakes rise, trust shifts toward:
- known brands
- official sources
- established publishers
- mainstream service brands
- familiar shortlists
- brands with proven track records

### Step 3: Premium is accepted only when it feels grounded
The TA accepts premium when it means:
- better materials
- better durability
- better service
- better ownership confidence
- better fit for adult life and the home

They are much less responsive to premium framed as:
- luxury for display
- trend status
- tech novelty
- inflated pricing without proof

### Step 4: Ownership matters as much as purchase
The TA repeatedly rewards:
- service
- durability
- easy support
- long-term dependability
- household fit
- practical confidence

This means trust is not only won at the moment of sale.  
It is strongly linked to how ownership is expected to feel afterward.

## 8. Working rules for later persona and semantic-core updates

### Trust rules
- mainstream familiarity is a strength, not a weakness
- official and authoritative proof matters more as stakes rise
- service is part of the product
- value must feel earned
- support and ownership reassurance are highly persuasive

### Premium rules
- premium should be quiet
- premium should feel durable
- premium should feel adult
- premium should feel grounded in use, not in status
- premium without practicality will underperform

### Risk rules
Avoid cues that suggest:
- flimsy quality
- disposable construction
- gimmick energy
- unexplained premium price
- flashy “look at me” branding
- weak support / unclear ownership experience

## 9. Final one-paragraph synthesis

Survey 1 shows a TA that is mainstream-trusting, quality-sensitive, and quietly premium in taste. They discover broadly, but when trust matters they narrow quickly toward familiar, proven brands, official sources, and dependable shortlists. They define quality through durability, service, and ownership confidence, not just aesthetics. They accept premium when it feels mature, understated, and genuinely worth owning over time. For ECHO, the winning trust posture is not flashy luxury or novelty tech, but **well-made, dependable, quietly premium, and easy to live with**.
